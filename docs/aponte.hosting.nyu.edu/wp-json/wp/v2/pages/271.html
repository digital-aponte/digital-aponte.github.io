When colonial Spanish officials searched Aponte’s home and workshop, they found his “book of paintings,” loose leaf images and documents, and also a small library. The library contained eleven books all \u201cold and used,\u201d recorded in the trial record as “one very elegant board book which is titled Descripci\u00f3n de Historia Natural<\/i>, an Arte Nebrija<\/i>, Gu\u00eda de Forasteros de la Isla de Cuba<\/i>, Maravillas de la Ciudad de Roma<\/i>, Estado Militar de Espa\u00f1a<\/i>, Sucesos Memorables del Mundo<\/i>, Historia del Conde Saxe<\/i>, Formulary for letter-writing<\/i>, Catecismo de la Doctrina Cristiana<\/i>, Vida del Sabio Hisopo <\/i>and the Third Volume of Don Quijote”\u00a0<\/i>(Pavez Ojeda 2006a, 727)<\/a>. In addition, Aponte testified that he had loaned two other books that he used in the making of the “book of paintings,” “El libro de la vida de San Antonio Abad and El Libro del Padre Fr. Luis Yrreta”\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2012, 273)<\/a>. Scholars Jorge Pavez Ojeda and Stephan Palmi\u00e9 have further identified possible versions of these books that Aponte owned. Click on any book spine in the image above to learn more. (We have left out of the above recreation the formulary for letter-writing.)<\/p>\n

During the trial testimony, Aponte and his assistant Trinidad Nu\u00f1ez, talk about using books that appear to have circulated amongst the community of free people of color. Aponte mentions learning from a \u201cbook of general history\u201d through a “black man whose name he did not remember\u201d who had arrived to Havana from Spain\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2006a, 734)<\/a>. Trinidad describes using the book by Father Luis Yrreta<\/a> to draw images on l\u00e1mina<\/em> 46<\/a> of the \u201cbook of paintings.\u201d He says the book was currently in the possession of the \u201cblack woman Catalina Gavilan in Guanabacoa\u201d\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2006a, 751)<\/a>, indicating Aponte had loaned it to her.\u00a0By 1791, seven printing presses operated in Havana, producing volumes like the Gu\u00eda de Forasteros de la Isla de Cuba<\/i><\/a>\u00a0that Aponte owned, but we remain unsure if he would have been able to purchase books from such presses. He may have relied instead on those community networks that spanned the city and, in the case of the man from Spain, the Atlantic.<\/p>\n

Jorge Pavez Ojeda singles out a few volumes from Aponte\u2019s library as influential in his conceptualization of the \u201cbook of paintings.\u201d First, he argues that volumes on historical Ethiopianism, including a history of Ethiopia<\/a> and volume on San Antonio Abad<\/a>, by scholars \u201cwho took it upon themselves to inform Europeans about the history of Abyssinia\u201d echoes Aponte\u2019s own mission in depicting and sharing his historical and artistic vision in colonial Havana (Pavez Ojeda 2012, 286)<\/a>. The authors of these books, Luis de Urreta and Juan de Baltasar, also actually appear represented in the book on l\u00e1mina<\/em> 29<\/a>, along with other scenes of Ethiopianist imagery. (A note: Stephan Palmi\u00e9 identifies the San Antonio Abad as instead being by Joseph Navarro.) Additionally, Aponte\u2019s assistant Trinidad N\u00fa\u00f1ez testified that he used these two books as sources to paint certain figures (Pavez Ojeda 2006a, 751)<\/a>.<\/p>\n

Secondly, Pavez Ojeda notes that the biography of Maurice of Saxe<\/a> likely inspired Aponte\u2019s many battle scenes, particularly because of \u201cthe prominence and fame of his battalion of black soldiers in the military successes of that well-known German freemason\u201d\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2012, 289)<\/a>.\u00a0As examples, Aponte represents his grandfather and father defending the city during the British invasion of 1762 on l\u00e1minas<\/em> 18-19<\/a> and the army of the African king Tarraco invading Hispania on l\u00e1minas<\/em> 24-25<\/a>.<\/p>\n

The volume of Aesop\u2019s Fables<\/a> also reflects Aponte\u2019s interest Greco-Roman mythology through his inclusion of a range of gods and goddesses in the \u201cbook of paintings.\u201d Pavez Ojeda also pinpoints the Aponte\u2019s uses of \u201caphorisms similar to those used by Aesop to denounce the ignominies of power,\u201d to a moral framework, as on l\u00e1mina<\/em> 20<\/a> where a phrase relates that “death cannot destroy prudence” alongside a depiction of the \u201cGod of fables\u201d\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2012, 291)<\/a>. Similarly, the early modern guide to Rome, Grandezas y maravillas de la inclyta y sancta ciudad de Roma<\/em><\/a>, perhaps impacted Aponte\u2019s own development of a \u201cmonumental urban cartography\u201d in his representations of locations and monuments in Havana(Pavez Ojeda 2006a, 754)<\/a>.<\/p>\n

Lastly, Aponte\u2019s possible ownership of Cuba’s first natural history<\/a>, by Portuguese naturalist Antonio Parra, provided a visual contrast to Aponte\u2019s own illustrious black history. Parra included in his volume three images of a free black man Domingo Fern\u00e1ndez, who suffered from elephantiasis, as an example of the limits of nature. Aponte\u2019s representations of black bodies offer heroic depictions in opposition to Parra\u2019s \u201cmonster\u201d\u00a0(Pavez Ojeda 2012, 296)<\/a>.<\/p>\n
